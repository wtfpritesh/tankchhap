Care & Maintenance

Three habits that make your umbrella last three monsoons.

Most umbrellas don't fail from rough use. They fail from how they're put away. Three small habits, learned over seventy-six years of making rain essentials, that turn a single-season buy into a five-year companion.

By BSC Editorial 1 May 2026 4 min read

Walk into our Indore workshop on a wet July morning and you'll see the same scene we've seen for decades: an umbrella brought back for repair, almost always with the same story. It only lasted one season. Nine times out of ten, the umbrella is fine. The frame is straight, the canopy is whole. What's failed is the spring mechanism, or the fabric near the ribs, and almost always for the same reason — it was put away wet, folded tight, and forgotten in a bag for a week.

Umbrellas are a remarkably forgiving product. They survive wind, hail, autorickshaw rides and being sat on. What they don't survive well is neglect at rest. Here are the three habits that make the difference between buying one umbrella every year and buying one every five.

One. Always dry it open.

This is the single most important habit, and it's the one almost no one does. When you come in from the rain, the instinct is to shake the water off, snap the umbrella shut, and stash it. Don't. Open the umbrella fully and stand it inverted (canopy down, handle up) in a corner for an hour or two until it's dry to the touch.

Here's why it matters: a folded umbrella stored wet traps moisture against the metal frame and the inside of the fabric. The frame begins to rust at the joints — which are the weakest, most stress-bearing parts. The fabric, especially in folded umbrellas where the canopy is bunched against itself, begins to mildew. Within two or three weeks of repeated wet-folding, you'll see brown spotting and the spring will start to feel sticky.

A folded umbrella stored wet is an umbrella shortening its own life every hour it sits there.

An open umbrella, dried in air, suffers none of this. It's the easiest habit on this list and the one with the biggest payoff.

Two. Close it gently. Twice.

Auto-open and two-fold umbrellas have a spring mechanism that's designed to be released, not slammed. When you close yours, do it in two motions instead of one: collapse the canopy first by hand, then ease the runner down the shaft. Don't push the button and let it crash closed — that compresses the spring violently against the catch, and over a few hundred uses it'll start to mis-engage.

The same principle applies to the fold. Once the canopy is collapsed, take a moment to align the panels along the ribs before wrapping the tie. A canopy folded with the panels twisted will develop creases that turn into tears at the rib tips. A canopy folded along the seams will look new for years.

A note on storage

If you carry your umbrella in a bag, get one with a separate compartment or sleeve. An umbrella loose in a bag rubs against keys, pen tips and laptop corners, and the fabric — even good polyester — wears through at the canopy edge first. The pouch your umbrella came in isn't decorative. Use it.

Three. Service the spring once a year.

Once a year, ideally before the monsoon arrives, take five minutes with your umbrella and a small tube of light machine oil (sewing-machine oil works perfectly). Open the umbrella fully. Put a single drop of oil where each rib meets the runner — the small joints that fold inward when the umbrella closes. Open and close the umbrella four or five times to work it in. Wipe off any excess.

This one habit, more than any other, separates a five-year umbrella from a one-year umbrella. The joints are where friction lives, and friction is where wear happens. A drop of oil takes thirty seconds and adds years.

The frame is what we build to last. You just have to keep the joints moving.

What about repairs?

If something does break — a rib bends, a spring loses tension, a panel tears — most of it is fixable. The umbrellas we make at BSC, T.V. and Tank Chhap are built around standard components precisely so that repair is possible. A bent rib can be straightened. A torn panel can be re-stitched. A broken spring can be replaced.

If you bought your umbrella from us and something has gone wrong with it, message us on WhatsApp with a photo. Most of the time we can tell you whether it's a five-minute fix or worth bringing in. We've been making umbrellas in central India since 1949 and we still believe a good umbrella is worth keeping.

The short version

  • Dry it open. Never store a wet umbrella folded.
  • Close it gently. Two motions, not one. Align the panels.
  • Oil the joints once a year. One drop per rib joint, before the season starts.

That's it. Three habits, maybe ten minutes of attention a year, and the umbrella you bought last May will still be with you in 2031.
